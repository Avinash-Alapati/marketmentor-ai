# Price-to-Earnings (PE) Ratio

## Definition

The Price-to-Earnings (PE) Ratio is one of the most widely used valuation metrics in investing. It measures how much investors are willing to pay for every ₹1 of a company's earnings.

A higher PE ratio generally indicates that investors expect higher future growth, while a lower PE ratio may indicate undervaluation or lower growth expectations.

---

## Formula

PE Ratio = Market Price per Share / Earnings Per Share (EPS)

---

## Example

Suppose:

- Market Price per Share = ₹600
- Earnings Per Share (EPS) = ₹30

PE Ratio = 600 ÷ 30 = 20

This means investors are paying ₹20 for every ₹1 of annual earnings.

---

## Interpretation

### High PE Ratio

A high PE ratio may indicate:

- Strong future growth expectations
- Premium valuation
- High investor confidence

Example:

Technology companies often trade at high PE ratios because investors expect rapid growth.

---

### Low PE Ratio

A low PE ratio may indicate:

- Undervalued stock
- Slower expected growth
- Company facing business challenges

A low PE does not automatically mean the stock is a good investment.

---

## Advantages

- Easy to calculate
- Useful for comparing companies in the same industry
- Helps estimate whether a stock is expensive or inexpensive
- Popular among investors and analysts

---

## Limitations

- Cannot be used when EPS is negative
- Should not be used to compare companies from different sectors
- Ignores debt and cash position
- Growth companies naturally have higher PE ratios

---

## Beginner Mistakes

- Comparing banks with IT companies
- Assuming low PE always means undervalued
- Ignoring company growth
- Ignoring industry average PE

---

## Practical Example

Company A

- Price = ₹800
- EPS = ₹40

PE = 20

Company B

- Price = ₹500
- EPS = ₹10

PE = 50

Although Company B is cheaper in price, investors are paying much more for its earnings.
