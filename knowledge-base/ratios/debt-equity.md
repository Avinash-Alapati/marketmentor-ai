# Debt-to-Equity Ratio

## Definition

The Debt-to-Equity (D/E) Ratio measures how much debt a company uses relative to shareholders' equity.

It helps investors understand the company's financial leverage and risk.

---

## Formula

Debt-to-Equity Ratio = Total Debt / Shareholders' Equity

---

## Example

Suppose:

- Total Debt = ₹400 Crore
- Shareholders' Equity = ₹800 Crore

Debt-to-Equity Ratio = 400 ÷ 800

Debt-to-Equity Ratio = 0.5

This means the company has ₹0.50 of debt for every ₹1 of equity.

---

## Interpretation

### Low Debt-to-Equity

- Lower financial risk
- Strong balance sheet
- Greater financial stability

### High Debt-to-Equity

- Higher financial risk
- Higher interest obligations
- Greater sensitivity during economic downturns

---

## Advantages

- Measures financial leverage
- Helps evaluate company risk
- Useful for comparing companies in the same industry

---

## Limitations

- Different industries have different ideal ratios
- Does not measure debt repayment ability
- Should not be used alone

---

## Practical Example

Company A

Debt-to-Equity = 0.45

Company B

Debt-to-Equity = 2.10

Company A has a much lower debt burden compared to Company B.

---

## Beginner Mistakes

- Assuming debt is always bad
- Comparing companies across industries
- Ignoring interest coverage ratio

## Key Takeaway

A moderate Debt-to-Equity Ratio generally indicates balanced financing. Excessively high debt increases financial risk, while too little debt may indicate underutilization of capital.
